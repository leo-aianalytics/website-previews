Our Story

A corner of 3rd Street that always has the lights on

There's a stretch of 3rd Street between 20th and 22nd where the sidewalk smells different in the morning. That's Neighbor Bakehouse. It opened quietly, the way good neighborhood spots usually do — no big fanfare, just a light on early and a line that started forming before most people had finished their first cup of coffee at home. Word got around the Dogpatch fast. Then it got around Potrero Hill. Then pretty much everywhere else.

What kept people coming back wasn't a gimmick or a seasonal menu designed for Instagram. It was the bread — the kind with a crust that makes a real sound when you tap it, and a crumb that pulls apart in a way that feels almost unfair. The bakers here treat dough like it deserves respect, which it does. Everything is made in small batches, which means some days you get the last croissant and feel genuinely lucky about it.

Over a thousand people have left reviews trying to explain what makes this place worth the early alarm. Most of them end up saying some version of the same thing: it just feels like your bakery, even the first time you walk in. You leave with a bag of something warm, flour maybe on the counter, and the vague sense that your whole morning got better. That's not a small thing. That's actually the whole thing.

What comes out of the oven

Made from scratch, in small batches, gone by afternoon — that's just how it works here

Breads & Loaves

These are the loaves people set their Saturday alarms for — sourdoughs and country breads with a crust that crackles and an inside that stays tender for days. Grab a whole loaf and it probably won't make it home intact.

Pastries & Morning Buns

The pastry case is the first thing you see when you walk in, and it has a way of making decisions for you. Croissants, morning buns, and rotating seasonal things that show up without announcement and disappear the same way.

Coffee & In-House Treats

There's no better reason to slow down than a good coffee and something baked that morning, eaten at a small table with nowhere to be. The counter staff will tell you what's fresh — and they actually mean it.
