Our Story

How a loaf of bread turned into something much bigger

It started with a line stretching down Guerrero Street and a sourdough loaf that people swore changed their lives. When Chad Robertson and Elisabeth Prueitt opened Tartine Bakery, they weren't trying to build an empire — they were trying to bake bread the right way, slowly, with good flour and real time. The Manufactory on Alabama Street came later, a few blocks east and a few sizes larger, but the obsession with craft never moved an inch.

Walk into 595 Alabama on a Tuesday morning and you'll see exactly what that obsession looks like in practice. There are bakers in the back working dough that's been fermenting since the day before. There are roasters pulling shots with the same seriousness a surgeon brings to an operating table. And there are pastry cooks building things — laminated things, layered things, things that involve more butter than you'd like to know about — that somehow still feel honest and unfussy when they land on your tray.

People come back because the Manufactory doesn't really feel like a restaurant or a bakery or a coffee shop — it feels like all three, on their best days, operating at the same time. You come in for a morning bun and end up staying for a pizza by the window at 7pm. You meant to grab one loaf and left with two because the person ahead of you in line said you'd regret it if you didn't. That's the thing about this place. It pulls you in and somehow, you always leave thinking you should have stayed a little longer.

"I've been coming here since they opened on Alabama and I still get a little excited walking through the door. The country loaf is the best bread I've ever had, full stop. I usually grab one on Saturday morning and it's gone by Sunday night. My only complaint is that Monday they're closed and I always forget."

What We Make Here

Bread, coffee, and dinner — made slowly, from scratch, every single day

Country Bread & Pastries

The whole-grain sourdough loaves that made Tartine famous are baked fresh daily and tend to disappear fast — there's a reason regulars plan their week around pickup. The pastry case runs alongside it with morning buns, kouign-amann, and seasonal tarts that make skipping breakfast feel like a personal failure.

Coffee & Espresso Bar

The coffee program here is taken just as seriously as the bread, with beans sourced and roasted with the same care that goes into every loaf. Whether you want a quiet cortado at the bar or an iced coffee to take up Alabama Street, they're not cutting corners on either end.

Evening Kitchen & Pizza

Tuesday through Sunday, the Manufactory shifts gears around dinnertime into something that feels like a proper sit-down meal — wood-fired pizzas, grain bowls, and plates that lean seasonal and satisfying without being precious about it. It's a genuinely great reason to come back after dark.
